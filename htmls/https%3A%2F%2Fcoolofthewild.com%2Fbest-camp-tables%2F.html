The Best Camp Tables: Luxury Camping Meals in 2020

The tent and stove may come first on your camping checklist but bringing a table will certainly add a new angle to your camp kitchen. You can forget about squatting over a precariously balanced camping stove, and the backache that comes with it. Pick the right camp table and you will never have to eat on the damp ground again!

There’s more to choosing a camping table then you might expect, though. You want to pick one that’s strong and stable but doesn’t weigh a ton. One that’s the right height for your chairs, and one that sets up and collapses in an instant.

Luckily for you, your friends here at Cool of the Wild have made the selection simple. We’ve checked out many types of outdoor tables and narrowed it down to what we think are the ten best camp tables in 2020.

KingCamp Bamboo Folding Table

Eco-conscious: Made from bamboo

Best for: Picnics and uneven ground

The Bamboo Folding Table from KingCamp has something that most camp tables lack: Adjustable legs. These can adjust from a height of 17” to 25.6” making it suitable for picnics on the ground or dining with chairs. There are no cross supports either, so you get plenty of legroom for two adults.

It may take a bit of practice to get the legs at the right length but the ability to adjust them individually will make the table more secure on uneven ground. We also like that the surface and legs fold together and fit into one carry case to minimise the possibility of losing parts.

The KingCamp Bamboo Folding Table is available in two sizes, the size and weight specifications above are for the smaller sized table. Both sizes are light and compact enough to be carried on a short walk.

Things to consider when choosing a camp table

Portability

The ability to move a camping table from A to B without too much hassle is essential. Most camp tables will fold to a size which fits comfortably inside the boot/trunk of a car, or, to a size which is reasonable to hike with. Most will come with a protective travel bag or a built-in carry handle. Likewise, the camp table you would take out adventuring should weigh as little as possible; below 2 lbs is ideal. Car campers, however, may trade a featherweight table for a larger and sturdier surface.

Set up

It’s also worth considering how quick your camping table is to set up. If you’re staying for a week in one campsite then half an hour to construct a table doesn’t seem like much. But if you’re moving camp every few days that’s going to bite into your playtime. The most portable camp tables take less than 5 minutes to set up. You shouldn’t need to be a rocket scientist or an Ikea salesman to work out how to put it together either!

Height

Think about what you’ll be using your table for. The best camp tables for cooking and food preparation should be tall enough to use while standing; the best for dining should leave enough room for your legs to fit underneath. If you camp with kids then a shorter frame might be preferable. The most versatile camp tables will come with an adjustable frame which allows you to alter the height.

Frame design

Camping tables with cross supports provide a stronger base and can often hold more weight. The counter side is that these supports take up leg room. If your main use is dining or playing cards, rather than cooking or holding luggage, then you might prefer a table without cross supports.

Some frames also have legs which adjust individually. This is a useful feature to have, unless your camping area comes with completely level ground.

Durability and materials

Are you just planning to put your coffee on the table and play cards or does your table need to support the weight of a family sized camping stove? If it’s the latter option then you’ll need one made from something stronger than plastic. Wood is much heavier but also more durable and more resistant to heat and scratches. In most cases, aluminium is the best material for a camp table. It’s lighter than wood, tougher than plastic, and resistant to weather, heat, and rust.

You’ll also need to decide between a hard-top and a soft-top table. As hard-tops are generally tougher and more stable, they’re usually preferable for cooking and dining. On the other hand, soft-tops tend to be compact and lightweight.
